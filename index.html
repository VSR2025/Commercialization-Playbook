The Commercialization
Playbook

Can you. Should you. How to.

Most companies sense they have something worth bringing to market. Few know how to tell. This playbook was built from real engagements — not from a distance, but inside the work. Scoping MVPs, validating with customers, making the go and no-go calls, and standing behind them. Two examples in this playbook came from enterprise clients. One went to market. One was advised not to — and was better for it. The discipline has been stress-tested against the Gen AI era. The questions got harder. The rigor got more important.

The Framework

Three gates. One rigorous decision.

1
Part One
Can You Productize?
+
Does this capability solve a problem better than anything out there today?
Can you measure the impact for a customer — in time saved, revenue gained, or cost reduced?
Are there people who would pay for this right now?
Could a Gen AI tool replicate what makes this capability special within the next 12 months?
2
Part Two
Should You Productize?
+

This gate examines whether productizing this capability puts your core business at risk. A yes here isn't automatically a no-go — but it demands honest weighing of tradeoffs.

Is this capability central to why your customers choose you over competitors?
Is this capability critical to growing your existing customer base?
Does this capability significantly reduce your own operational costs?
Does the natural cadence of this use case support a viable business model?
3
Part Three
How to Go to Market
+

You've passed the gates. Now see the whole board.

These are not yes/no questions — they are thinking prompts. The quality of your answers determines how well you build, not whether you build.

Lens 01
Desirability
Do the right people want this — urgently enough to act?
1
Who specifically is your customer?
Name the buyer, the role, and the moment they feel this problem most acutely. Not a segment — a person in a specific situation.
Ask yourself — is this specific enough that someone who doesn't know your business could picture exactly who you mean?
2
Does your product integrate into your customer's life seamlessly — or do they have to change their behavior or habits to use it?
Grammarly didn't win by being better — it won by showing up inside every writing surface so users never had to leave their workflow to use it. The more behavior change you require, the stronger your value proposition needs to be.
Ask yourself — if your customer has to form a new habit to use this, what makes that worth it to them specifically?
3
Is this a problem people are actively trying to solve right now — or one they've learned to live with?
A great product solving a problem people can live with will always struggle. A good-enough product solving a problem that keeps people up at night will find its way. The urgency lives in the problem, not the solution.
Ask yourself — if your answer sounds like a vitamin, go back to Part 1 and pressure-test your early adopter signal before you proceed.
4
What is pushing them away from their current solution?
Name the specific frustration, cost, or limitation that makes the status quo unacceptable. This is your wedge into the market.
Ask yourself — the sharper your answer here, the sharper your value proposition becomes. Vague push leads to vague positioning.
5
What is pulling them toward your product — and is that pull strong enough to make them switch?
What do they imagine will be different when they use it? The pull has to be compelling enough to overcome the inertia of switching — which is always higher than expected.
Ask yourself — are you selling a category or a specific outcome? The more specific the promised outcome, the more compelling the switch.
Lens 02
Feasibility
Can you actually build and deliver this — at the quality the market expects?
1
What does it take to build and deliver this?
People, technology, infrastructure — be honest about what you have and what you need. The gaps you don't name now become the ones that delay your launch.
Ask yourself — if you were honest about the gaps, what is the single most critical one to close before you can ship?
2
How will you bring this to market — what do you build yourself, what do you buy, and where do you need partners?
Name each component and the path it takes. Then ask — what breaks in your plan if a key dependency disappears? Vendor dependency is a real strategic risk that is easy to underestimate early.
Ask yourself — which dependency, if it disappeared tomorrow, would stop your business?
3
What are you committing to innovate?
Most companies default to product feature innovation. The ones that build durable businesses often innovate on business model, customer experience, or distribution instead. Use Doblin's Ten Types of Innovation to be precise about where your real innovation lives.
Ask yourself — if your innovation type isn't clear to you, it won't be clear to your customer or your team.
4
Do you have a plan to deliver reliable, consistent outcomes for customers?
If your product is AI-powered, output is probabilistic by nature. What guardrails, testing, and quality monitoring ensure your customers get consistent results? If your product is not AI-powered, consider what operational consistency looks like at scale.
Ask yourself — what happens to your customer relationship the first time your product delivers an inconsistent outcome?
5
Given your current resources and timeline, what can you build and ship that delivers real value — and what are you explicitly leaving out?
A good MVP is defined as much by what it doesn't do as by what it does. Scoping with constraints forces clarity on what actually matters to your first customers.
Ask yourself — show your 'not in' list to your most enthusiastic early adopter. If they push back hard on something you excluded, that is a signal worth listening to.
Lens 03
Viability
Does a sustainable business exist here — not just a product?
1
What will customers pay for — and how?
Subscription, transaction, outcome, project — name the model and why it fits how this customer actually buys. Pricing to the value you deliver is always stronger than pricing to what it cost you to build.
Ask yourself — could you explain this pricing model to your customer's CFO in one sentence and have them immediately see why it's fair?
2
How often will customers use this — and does your pricing model match that rhythm?
Daily use, monthly, annually — the rhythm of need determines what model is sustainable. A use case that happens rarely isn't unviable. It just needs a different model than subscription.
Ask yourself — if a customer skips a cycle, does your revenue model survive that? Model it before you commit.
3
Are you pricing for the value you deliver — or just defaulting to the easiest model?
Per-seat pricing is a common default and often the wrong one — especially when your product replaces work that previously required many people. Price to value, not to convention.
Ask yourself — could your customer explain to their CFO exactly what they're paying for and why it's worth it?
4
Do you have a rough sense of what it costs to acquire, serve, and retain a customer — and does the math work at your target price?
You don't need a precise financial model at this stage — but you need a directional sense of whether the economics work. Rough numbers now prevent expensive surprises later.
Ask yourself — what is your break-even customer count, and do you have a credible path to getting there?
5
What does success look like — for you and for your customer — and are they aligned?
If your definition of success and your customer's definition of success are different things, you have a product-market fit problem waiting to happen. The best product businesses win when the customer's outcome and your revenue are the same event.
Ask yourself — if you can't describe your customer's success in their language, you don't know it well enough yet.
Lens 04
Defensibility
What makes this hard to take from you — now and over time?
1
Do you own the customer relationship directly — or does someone stand between you and your customer?
A channel partner, a platform, a marketplace — if someone else owns the relationship, your defensibility is only as strong as that intermediary's loyalty to you. Distribution partnerships accelerate growth but don't confuse them with owning the customer.
Ask yourself — know the difference between a distribution channel and a customer relationship. One accelerates growth. The other is your business.
2
What would it cost your customer — in time, money, or effort — to walk away after a year?
Switching costs are often stickier in workflow and habit than in technology. The higher the cost of leaving, the stronger your retention moat — but the best switching costs are designed in, not hoped for.
Ask yourself — are you actively designing switching costs in, or hoping stickiness emerges on its own?
3
Where does the uniqueness of this capability actually live?
Data, algorithm, process discipline, expertise, or content — be honest. The source of your uniqueness determines how defensible you truly are and how long that defensibility lasts.

4
Can you design this product so that value compounds as more customers use it (network effects)?
Network effects don't have to be social. Data flywheels, learning loops, and industry benchmarks are all forms of compounding value. The question is whether more customers make your product better — or just bigger.
Ask yourself — if your product doesn't get better with more users, is there a version of it that could? That design decision is worth making early.
5
Is the experience emotionally engaging — or purely functional?
Functional value is the easiest for competitors to replicate. Emotional engagement — trust, identity, relief, community — is much harder to copy. Pure utility is a race to the bottom on price and features.
Ask yourself — beyond the functional job, what emotional job does your product do? If you can't name it, your brand will stay generic.
